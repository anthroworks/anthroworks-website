About AnthroWorks.

A living space
for inquiry

Born from post-apartheid Durban, South Africa, AnthroWorks exists at the intersection of people, place, and practice. We are a research-led platform grounded in lived experience — attentive to the ways memory, identity, and belonging are shaped by geography, history, and community.

The Founder

Chad Payne

Strategist, designer, builder. Trained in Visual Communication Design at Stellenbosch University, with a research foundation in anthropology and cultural studies. Based in Cape Town.

AnthroWorks is how I hold together two instincts: the academic impulse to understand the world deeply, and the practical drive to make things that work. The practice runs as a small research studio — publications, consumer AI products led by IZWI, and strategy work with organisations — all informed by the same cultural inquiry.

I work as a solo founder, supported by a considered stack of AI tools that carry the rhythm of the practice — archives, correspondence, early drafts — so the thinking, the research, and the making can stay in human hands.

What We Believe

We believe that the most important knowledge about a place comes from the people who live there. That cultural research should be done with communities, not about them. That African knowledge systems — Ubuntu, indigenous ecology, oral tradition — contain design methodologies the world needs.

We’re not trend-driven. We’re not purely academic. We’re not commercial storytelling for its own sake. We choose depth, curiosity, and responsibility.

Ubuntu: I Am Because We Are

Ubuntu philosophy anchors everything we do. “I am because we are” is not a slogan — it is a relational epistemology that recognises meaning, identity, and creativity emerge between people, not within isolated individuals.

This shapes how we think about knowledge: ideas emerging from AnthroWorks are co-created and held in common. We are stewards of this space, not proprietors of knowledge. Contributions benefit the collective. Attribution is shared. Insights belong to the commons.

Three Orientations

  • Observation We begin by attending to context with care — listening deeply, noticing patterns, honouring what is already present before imposing frameworks.
  • Participation We engage with communities authentically, recognising that understanding emerges through relationship, not extraction. This is not fieldwork. It is co-presence.
  • Re-imagining We hold space for what might emerge when we resist closure and remain curious. Re-imagining is not about utopian visions but about staying with questions long enough for new possibilities to surface.

AI as Medium

In 2026, AnthroWorks doesn’t only study how technology shapes culture — we work with it. AI is our medium: a tool for archives, drafting, and the quiet administration that keeps a small practice running. We are transparent about where it sits and where it doesn’t. Research, relationships, making, and the calls that matter remain human.

We believe the most meaningful AI products will be built by people who understand culture, not just technology. That’s the thesis behind everything we do.

Our North Star

No matter the success, commercial pressure, or institutional partnerships, these commitments will not change:

  • Depth over frequency We publish slowly and with care. Building genuine, community-centred work is a multi-year journey — not a content mill optimised for visibility.
  • Calm over clever Our voice is measured, our work is considered, and our authority is built through substance rather than volume.
  • The collective over the individual Ubuntu remains the foundation. Knowledge is held in common, not extracted for profit. The relational DNA of this platform is sacred.
