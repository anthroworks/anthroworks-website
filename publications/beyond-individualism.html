Beyond Individualism: Ubuntu in Contemporary Practice

Rethinking creative and research practices through the lens of interdependence, community, and shared becoming.

Ubuntu is one of the most frequently invoked concepts in contemporary South African discourse. Politicians use it in speeches. Corporations embed it in mission statements. Design thinking workshops reference it as a framework for empathy. And yet, for all its visibility, Ubuntu remains remarkably underexplored as an operational methodology — a way of actually doing research, making creative work, and building institutions.

This article asks what happens when we move Ubuntu from philosophy to practice. Not as a branding exercise, but as a genuine restructuring of how knowledge is produced, shared, and owned.

What Ubuntu Actually Means

The isiZulu/isiXhosa expression “umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu” — a person is a person through other people — is the most commonly cited articulation of Ubuntu. But this translation, while true, only scratches the surface. Ubuntu is not merely a statement about empathy or social connection. It is an ontological claim: that the self is constituted through relationships, that individuality is an abstraction, and that human flourishing is inherently collective.

“Ubuntu is not a nice idea about being kind to each other. It is a radical claim about the nature of reality: that existence itself is relational.”

This distinction matters enormously for practice. If Ubuntu is simply about empathy, then it can be bolted onto existing Western frameworks — a “human-centred” design principle, a corporate value, a feel-good slogan. But if Ubuntu is an ontological position — a fundamentally different account of what it means to exist — then it requires fundamentally different structures for research, creation, and knowledge production.

The Individualism of Western Research

Western research practice is built on individual authorship. The single-author paper. The named researcher. The principal investigator. The intellectual property regime that assigns ownership of ideas to individuals. Even collaborative research is structured through clearly delineated individual contributions — who did what, who thought what first, who gets credit.

This framework is not culturally neutral. It reflects a specific philosophical tradition — one that begins with the Cartesian cogito and extends through liberal political theory to contemporary intellectual property law. It assumes that ideas originate in individual minds and are then shared with others. Ubuntu suggests the opposite: that knowledge emerges from the spaces between people, and that claiming individual ownership of collectively produced insight is a category error.

Ubuntu as Research Methodology

What would Ubuntu-informed research actually look like in practice? Several principles emerge:

Shared authorship as default. Not as a generous gesture, but as an accurate reflection of how knowledge is produced. When a researcher enters a community and leaves with insights, those insights were co-produced. The community members who shared their knowledge are not “informants” or “participants” — they are co-authors.

Process over output. In an Ubuntu framework, the relationships built during research are as valuable as the research outputs. A project that strengthens community bonds but produces no publishable paper may be more successful, by Ubuntu standards, than one that generates high-impact publications but leaves the community unchanged or depleted.

Collective benefit as measure of rigour. Western research measures rigour through methodological precision, sample size, and peer review. Ubuntu-informed research might add a further criterion: does the research benefit the community from which it emerged? If knowledge is extracted but not returned, the research has failed — regardless of its methodological sophistication.

Creative Practice and Ubuntu

The implications for creative practice are equally profound. The Western art world is built on the myth of individual genius — the solitary artist whose unique vision produces original work. Ubuntu suggests a different model: creative practice as communal activity, where the artwork emerges from collective processes and belongs, in some meaningful sense, to the community that produced it.

This is not a theoretical abstraction. Across Southern Africa, creative traditions have always operated this way. Beadwork traditions encode collective knowledge that no individual “invented.” Song traditions carry melodies and lyrics that have been refined across generations. Architectural practices — from the decorated homes of the Ndebele to the rondavel forms found across the region — represent collective design intelligence accumulated over centuries.

The Challenge for Institutions

Moving Ubuntu from philosophy to practice requires institutional change. Universities would need to rethink authorship, assessment, and intellectual property policies. Galleries would need to reconsider how they attribute and compensate creative work. Research funders would need new metrics for evaluating project success. None of this is easy. All of it is necessary.

AnthroWorks is committed to this work — not as an external observer cataloguing Ubuntu’s theoretical implications, but as a practice that attempts to operationalise its principles in our own research, creative collaborations, and institutional structures. We are learning, failing, and trying again. Which, perhaps, is itself the most Ubuntu thing we can do.

Chad J. Payne Founder & Research Director, AnthroWorks
