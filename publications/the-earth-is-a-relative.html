The Earth is a Relative: Decolonising the Post-Human in African Art and Anthropology

How African ontologies challenge the Western separation of human and nature — and what this means for art, design, and ecological futures.

In Western philosophical tradition, the boundary between human and non-human has been drawn with exceptional confidence. Descartes gave us the thinking subject, distinct from the mechanical world. The Enlightenment codified nature as resource, landscape as property, animals as automata. Even the contemporary “post-human” turn — which claims to decenter the human — often does so from within frameworks that never questioned the original separation.

African ontologies offer a fundamentally different starting point. In many African knowledge systems, the Earth is not a resource to be managed. It is a relative. A participant. A co-author of human life.

The Problem with “Post-Humanism”

The post-human turn in Western academia — drawing on thinkers like Donna Haraway, Bruno Latour, and Rosi Braidotti — has productively challenged anthropocentric thinking. But it has done so largely without engaging the knowledge systems that never held anthropocentric positions in the first place.

“You cannot decolonise the post-human by reading more European philosophy. You decolonise it by listening to the communities who never separated themselves from the Earth.”

This is not a minor scholarly oversight. It represents a structural pattern in which African, Indigenous, and Global South epistemologies are treated as data to be theorised about, rather than as theoretical frameworks in their own right. The irony is sharp: a movement dedicated to decentering the human continues to center European intellectual traditions.

African Ontologies of Relation

Across the African continent, diverse cosmologies share a common thread: the insistence on relational existence. The Zulu concept of ukuhlonipha (respect/reverence) extends not only to elders and ancestors but to the land, to water, to animals. The Shona understanding of nyika encompasses not just territory but the spiritual and ecological totality of place. In Yoruba cosmology, orí (personal destiny) is understood as embedded within a web of relationships that includes the non-human world.

These are not quaint cultural beliefs to be catalogued by anthropologists. They are sophisticated ontological positions — theories of being — that address the precise questions Western post-humanism is now asking, often with greater subtlety and practical application.

Art as Ontological Practice

Several contemporary African artists work explicitly within these relational ontologies. Their art does not merely depict human-nature relationships; it enacts them.

El Anatsui’s monumental metal tapestries — woven from discarded bottle caps and copper wire — collapse distinctions between waste and beauty, industrial material and natural form. The works breathe, shift, and transform with each installation, refusing the fixity that Western aesthetics typically demands. They are, in a very real sense, alive.

Wangechi Mutu’s hybrid figures — part human, part plant, part machine — visualise a world where categorical boundaries have dissolved. Drawing on both Kenyan cultural traditions and Afrofuturist aesthetics, her work imagines bodies that are already post-human in the truest sense: not transcending the natural world but merging with it.

Implications for Anthropological Practice

If we take African relational ontologies seriously — not as “perspectives” but as genuine accounts of how the world works — then anthropology itself must change. The discipline can no longer study “culture” as something exclusively human. It must account for the agencies of rivers, soils, forests, and climates as participants in cultural production.

This is where AnthroWorks positions itself. Our research does not begin with human subjects in isolation. It begins with the relationships — between people, place, ecology, and the non-human world — from which culture emerges. The Earth is not our field site. It is our relative. And that changes everything about how we work.

Chad J. Payne Founder & Research Director, AnthroWorks
